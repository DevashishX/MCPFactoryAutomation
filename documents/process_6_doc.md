# Low-Temperature Sensitive Components Assembly Process

## Process Overview

**Process Name:** Low-Temperature Sensitive Components Assembly

**Date Invented:** November 2010

**Invented By:** Bosch Sensortec

**Industry Application:** MEMS Sensors, Precision Instruments, Medical Diagnostics

## Process Steps

1. Low-temperature solder paste application for thermal protection
2. High-precision component placement with micro-positioning
3. Gentle reflow soldering at 235C with controlled ramp rates
4. 3D automated optical inspection for micro-defect detection
5. In-circuit testing without mechanical stress application

## Process Description

The Low-Temperature Sensitive Components Assembly process was developed by Bosch Sensortec in 2010 to address the growing demand for electronics incorporating delicate MEMS accelerometers, gyroscopes, pressure sensors, and other micro-electromechanical devices that can be damaged by excessive thermal exposure. This process utilizes specialized low-temperature solder paste formulations, typically bismuth-tin or indium-based alloys with melting points between 138C and 180C, protecting sensitive components from thermal shock and mechanical stress induced by coefficient of thermal expansion mismatches. The paste is applied with precise volume control to accommodate the fine-pitch leads and small pad geometries common in sensor packages. High-precision placement systems equipped with micro-positioning capabilities handle miniature components measuring as small as 0.4mm Ã— 0.2mm, ensuring accurate alignment critical for maintaining sensor calibration and performance specifications.

The reflow soldering phase employs carefully controlled temperature ramp rates, typically limited to 2-3C per second, and maintains a peak temperature of just 235C with minimal time above liquidus to prevent damage to polymer-based MEMS structures, piezoelectric crystals, and hermetic seal integrity. Following assembly, 3D automated optical inspection systems examine solder joint formation at microscopic levels, detecting issues such as micro-voids, fillet irregularities, or component tilt that could affect sensor accuracy. The in-circuit testing methodology uses specialized low-force probes and non-invasive measurement techniques to verify electrical functionality without applying mechanical stress that could alter sensor calibration. This gentle yet precise process is essential for manufacturing fitness trackers, smartphone motion sensors, tire pressure monitoring systems, and medical diagnostic equipment where sensor accuracy and long-term stability are paramount.
